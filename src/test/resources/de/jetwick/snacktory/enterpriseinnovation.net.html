Big Data to survive the intensifying squeeze of regulatory compliance

By Jason Demby, ‎director of Business Development of Financial Services, Datameer | 2016-05-10
 Email PrintShare
Caption: 
Jason Demby, ‎director of Business Development of Financial Services, Datameer

Today’s financial industry is experiencing immense amounts of pressure on all sides. The squeeze of high regulation is pushing banks towards low risk, low margin lines of business. Competitive pressures, including those from upstart Fintech companies, are challenging financial services companies to provide innovative customer experiences, while also ensuring that they meet compliance requirements and adhere to the latest alphabet soup of regulations coming down the pipeline. In the face of intensifying competition, legacy financial services institutions are lobbying for Fintech competitors to receive additional regulatory scrutiny in order to even the playing field.

Each new industry regulation tightens the vice on the corporate data architecture; and the pressure is building. It’s enough pressure to make anyone, especially IT managers, blow a gasket. As regulations pour in, IT must work to improve big data architectures to meet risk reporting, data aggregation and governance requirements. As regulations increase, organizations are forced to devote more and more of their annual budgets to supporting IT.

IT managers are tasked with developing big data architectures that can handle constantly changing requirements, offer robust data integration and transformation, and provide governance for big data discovery.

The next turn of the screw

The most difficult aspect of regulatory compliance is that each requirement creates a different set of pressures. It makes selecting the right data architecture an arduous task. Financial services data, for instance, comes in a number of classifications including client and product reference data, transactional data, security data, and more. Within an organization, it is likely that a different team manages and analyzes each type of data. When a new regulation makes a splash, all of these different teams must work (often in silos) to ensure their data storage, processing, and analytics adhere to the proper compliance requirements. Sometimes that requires the creation of an entirely new set of analytics and reporting. Regardless of the solution, it’s fairly clear there isn’t a one-size-fits-all method for dealing with the constant churn of the changing compliance landscape.

For example, the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision’s (BCBS) BCBS 239 regulation governs data aggregation and risk reporting. It stipulates that bank risk reports include a veritable mountain of risk data analysis for which financial institutions must be compliant. The list includes a wide array of risk types with varying datasets and requirements. To comply with each and every different scenario is a meticulous, and often costly, process.

With this is mind, organizations need to consider solutions that can consolidate different data types. To do so, they need to select a big data architecture designed for variety, flexibility, and efficiency.

Withstanding the pressure – with agility

A big data solution must be able to gather and analyze both structured and unstructured data from a variety of sources. The need to apply schemas to unstructured data before completing analysis can cause more headaches than solutions. Flexible data integration will help streamline the analysis process, without requiring organizations to know exactly what questions they want to answer beforehand. The infrastructure must also provide enterprise-level governance including a permissions hierarchy to maintain data consistency. For example, teams across an organization should have access to the specific sets of data that they need to analyze. In an ideal solution, data analysis should be easily completed by one group and then passed on to other groups who rely on the data as part of downstream analysis, all while maintaining the “true” data source.

For financial institutions, big data is a growing concern. Financial companies need to harness the potential of big data for business growth, while also meeting compliance requirements, which is no small task.

When striving for quick, actionable and compliant insights, a financial institution should look into a proper balance of architectural flexibility and robust governance to ensure that it doesn’t crumble under the next pressure.
